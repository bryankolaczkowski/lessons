bacterial ecology and human health

the "microbiome" and human health

Bacteria impact our health, and not always in a bad way.

When we think about bacteria and human health, the mind almost always turns to pathogens, first. While pathogenic microbes certainly have strong impacts on health, microbial communities can produce much more subtle effects that contribute to health and disease.

In general, science has a healthy appreciation for the many roles microbial communities have and continue to play on earth. It is widely appreciated that the evolution of microbes has gone hand-in-hand with broad changes in earth bio-geo-chemistry; microbes have shaped the planet over billions of years. Microbial communities continue to play key roles in global chemical and nutrient cycling and contribute to weather and climate processes on a global scale.

Microbial communities also play key roles in plant and animal health. Of course, the most 'obvious' impact of microbes on plant and animal health is as causes of pathogenic disease. But it's also safe to say that plant and animal biology would not be the same without associated microbial communities. Soil and root-associated microbes play key roles in plant metabolism, and gut-associated microbes are important for digestion and gut health in humans and other animals.

While it is obvious that microbial communities are critically important for the proper functioning of 'higher' organisms, and for the functioning of earth's biosphere, what is less well understood is precisely how microbial communities impact these larger processes.

One of the central goals of the field of 'microbial ecology' is to better understand how the structure and dynamics of microbial communities impacts the functioning of 'larger scale' processes.

In the case of microbial pathogens, the 'connection' between the microbial community and the 'larger scale' process of human disease can be relatively simple: if you 'get' the pathogen, you 'get' the disease.

In other cases, however, the 'connection' between the microbial community and a complex human disease can be much more complex.

For example, it is now widely appreciated that the community of microbes living in the human gut plays a key role in the development of the human immune system, probably via direct interactions with the gut epithelial cells during early development. If these interactions work 'correctly', the human immune system develops 'appropriately'. However, if something goes 'wrong' with this process, the human immune system can develop 'incorrectly', leading to a wide variety of autoimmune disorders.

An example of a human autoimmune disorder associated with the gut microbial community is type 1 diabetes (T1D). The 'cause' of type 1 diabetes is destruction of insulin-producing B-cells in the pancreas by the body's own immune system. Once B-cells are destroyed by autoimmunity, the body can no longer produce insulin, which is required for human life. At this point, the individual is completely dependent on exogenous insulin.

In contrast to the much more prevalent type 2 diabetes (which has an entirely different cause), type 1 diabetes primarily effects children. In additoin to genetic and environmental risk factors, it is thought that the structure of the gut microbial community during early childhood may have a strong impact on an individual's risk for developing T1D. However, exactly how the gut microbial community may contribute to T1D risk is not understood.

There is a robust international research effort aimed at characterizing the structure and dynamics of the human gut-associated microbial community in 'healthy' subjects, and comparing 'healthy' gut microbial communities to those found in subjects who will develop type 1 diabetes, in order to better understand how changes in the structure and dynamics of the gut "microbiome" might impact disease risk, onset and progression.

This is only one example of how researchers are attempting to untangle how microbial ecology might causally impact larger-scale processes. Whatever the particular larger-scale process under study, the structure of the research program is very similar: characterize the structure of the microbial community, and then determine which changes in microbial community structure are associated with observed differences in the larger-scale process.

characterizing microbial communities

The 16S rDNA paradigm.

Scientists have come up with multiple approaches for characterizing the structure of microbial communities. One of the most commonly used methods relies on amplifying and sequencing a 'universal' region of the bacterial genome, which can be used to organize and compare bacterial 'species' within a sample.

The specific 'universal marker' most commonly used in bacterial community sequencing is a specific region of the 16S rDNA gene, which encodes a component of the bacterial ribosome. The characteristics of this particular 'marker' that make it 'good' for characterizing bacterial community composition are:

  • It is 'universal' - ie, the 16S rDNA gene is found in (nearly?) all bacterial species.
  • It provides good 'resolution' - nearly all unique bacterial 'species' can be identified using the 16S rDNA sequence, and the sequence is not too different to prevent comparisions among very distantly-related bacteria.

In the absence of morphological, genomic or other data, sequencing a specific 'universal' region of the bacterial 16S rDNA gene allows researchers to fairly confidently identify particular bacterial 'species' in a biological sample. In other words, 16S rDNA sequencing provides a 'marker' that can be used to 'cluster' bacteria in a sample into different 'species' (or some other taxonomic level appropriate for the specific study goals).

It's important to note that no particular 'marker' is perfect, and there is much debate concerning the utility of the 16S rDNA sequencing approach, both in general and in particular use cases. Scientific debate notwithstanding, it's pretty safe to say that the vast majority of existing bacterial community studies use a 16S rDNA sequencing approach, and this approach is likely to dominate the majority of future studies, at least for a few more years.

It's also important to note that 16S rDNA sequencing provides researchers with an estimate of 'which' particular 'species' of bacteria is present in a sample, but not necessarily with information about what that bacterium is actually doing in the community. For this reason, 16S rDNA sequencing studies are confined to characterizing the species composition of the microbial community; they cannot directly investigate the functioning of bacterial communities or their individual components.

The information or data you get out of a 16S rDNA sequencing study are a 'list' of bacterial 'species' and the relative abundance of each species in a particular biological sample. In practice, the 'species' in a sample may or may not correspond to the Linnaean classification system. Depending on the study design, you might get genus-species 'names' for the bacteria in the sample, or you might just get 'operational taxonomic units', with no clear connection to known "species".

In any case, the 16S rDNA analysis provides you with which species are present in a sample, as well as how many (relatively!) of each species is present. From this information, you can determine who the 'dominant players' are in a given sample's bacterial community, as well as which bacteria might also be present, but in lower numbers. By comparing the species compositions across different samples, scientists can start to better understand how microbial community composition changes in response to different environmental 'features', such as time, nutrient levels, or disease/health of the 'host' species.

Recently, there has been quite a bit of research interest in the idea that we might be able to use bacterial species composition to help 'predict' an individual's disease risk. For example, if the human gut microbial community impacts type 1 diabetes risk, could we sequence an individual's gut microbial community in early childhood, and then use this information to better predict the individual's risk of later developing T1D? If we could reliably predict T1D risk early, could we develop and deploy interventions to help prevent later development of the disease?

Similar to the way traditional genomic phenotype 'prediction' methods work, traditional approaches used to study how bacterial communities impact larger-scale processes have largely consisted of examining the statistical association between each individual microbial taxon and the process under investigation, and then finding which particular microbial taxa are more strongly associated with observed differences in the larger-scale process than would be expected, based on chance.

These types of simple 'association' studies are well-suited to identifying particular strongly-associated bacterial 'species', which can then be isolated and studied in the lab.

But what if the association between the gut microbial community and disease risk is not simple or driven by a relatively small number of individual 'species'? If the 'real' causal links between the gut microbial community and disease risk are complex and involve interactions among a relatively large number of 'species', they could easily be missed by traditional approaches that only consider each species in isolation.

To address this issue, researchers are turning to increasingly complex statistical modeling approaches, like deep neural networks, to attempt to 'capture' complex nonlinear interactions among bacterial species that could impact larger-scale processes like autoimmune disease risk.

While complex statistical modeling approaches have the potential to shed new light on how microbial community structure impacts its function in the environment, these methods are known to require very large training data sets to be reliable. And although 16S rDNA sequencing costs continue to fall dramatically, sampling microbial communities - whether in the deep ocean or in the human gut - remains difficult and expensive at the scale needed to reliably train deep neural networks.
