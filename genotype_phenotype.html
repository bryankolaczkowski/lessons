PHENOTYPE PREDICTION

your genome impacts how you look and act... but how?

What is phenotype prediction?

Predicting how you look and act, based on your genome and environment.

We all look and act a little differently. We have different heights, hair and eye colors, blood types, etc. We also respond differently to our environment. We might require different amounts of sleep, have different natural energy levels, have allergies to different foods, respond differently to pharmaceuticals, etc.

Collectively, how an organism looks and acts can be referred to as the organism's "phenotype". The phenotype generally includes everything you can measure about an organism's morphology, physiology and behavior.

There are two major factors that impact an organism's phenotype:

  • its genome
  • its environment
An organism's genome is its DNA inherited from its parents and modified by mutations. Its environment is everything else.

Typically, we think of the genomic component as the heritable factors that impact the way an organism looks and acts. The genome is 'passed on' (with modification) from parent to offspring. If the genome strongly determines a particular aspect of the phenotype (called a "trait"), that trait is heritable; it can be 'inherited' from the organism's parents.

More 'quantitatively', we can talk about the "heritability" of a phenotypic trait - the extent to which the trait is determined by the organism's genome. Or, more broadly, the extent to which the child's phenotype is 'similar to' the phenotype of its parents (after controlling for any environmental determination).

Phenotype prediction is using information about an organism's genome and environment to predict a specific quantitative or qualitative aspect of its phenotype.

For example, let's say we'd like to predict a person's height. The response variable is "height", a 'quantitative trait' (ie, a number). The potential explanatory variables might be the person's genome and information about the person's current and past environments, such as the country the person grew up in, the person's income, information about their dietary and exercise habits, and any diseases they may have or may have had in the past.

We can then build a model relating the explanatory variables (genome, environment) to the response variable (height). The model is just a function: 

height = f(genome, environment)

Which can be a simple linear model or any arbitrary function that can be approximated by a neural network.

Given enough 'training examples' (people with known height, genome and enviornmental variables), we can 'learn' the 'trainable parameters' of the model.

Once trained, we can use the model to predict any individual's height from their genome and environment.

In this example, we used both the genome and the environment to predict an individual's phenotype. This would be a case of 'general' phenotype prediction.

In many cases, researchers are interested in predicting the phenotype using only the organism's genome; this is sometimes called "genomic prediction" (which is maybe weird, because we are not trying to predict the genome!). Phenotype prediction using only the genome is typically much more straight-forward than trying to deal with data about the individual's environment, which can be complex and difficult to measure and analyze effectively. Predicting an organism's phenotypic traits from its genome has many important applications throughout the agricultural and life sciences.

How is phenotype prediction used?

In medical research, agriculture and basic biology.

Medical Research

There are two primary applications of phenotype prediction in the biomedical sciences

  • disease risk prediction
  • personalized medicine

Disease risk prediction is the prediction of an individual's risk for developing a particular disease, based on the person's genome. In this case, the 'response variable' is the risk of the person developing the disease. The 'explanatory variables' are the person's genome, which is typically encoded by listing the specific DNA bases at a large number of sites across the genome which are known to vary in the human population. Theoretically, we could use the person's entire genomic sequence as explanatory variables, but reducing the ~3.2 billion bases in the human genome to ~1million or so 'marker' sites saves a lot of computation and data storage without sacrificing accuracy very much.

We can build a model for predicting disease risk from genomic sequence markers 

disease_risk = f(genome)

where the function f can be any function we'd like. For example, a simple linear model would be: 

disease_risk = M * genome + b

where M is a vector of slopes (one for each base in the genome), and b is the bias term. This model has one slope parameter for every position in the genome (ie, its 'dimension' is the number of positions in the genome), plus a single bias parameter. And yes, this would be implemented using a single densely-connected neuron!

Once trained on a broad sample of people with known disease-risk and known genomic sequence, we could use this model to predict the disease risk of a new individual, based only on their genome sequence. Because an individual's genome is know at birth (or before), predicting disease risk from genome sequence can be used to develop very early treatment strategies that can help mitigate disease risk long before any symptoms or clinical markers might be present.

You should note that phenotype prediction is different from a "genome wide association study" (GWAS), the goal of which is to identify which specific genomic variants are most strongly associated with disease risk. GWAS is all about identifying genomic variants. Disease risk prediction, on the other hand, is all about accurately predicting disease risk, and it doesn't really care which specific genomic variants contribute to that risk. Although you can extract both types of information from both types of studies (you can predict disease risk from GWAS, and you can identify risk-associated variants from phenotype prediction), they start from fundamentally different places and have different primary goals.

Personalized medicine is the tailoring of medical treatment to the individual patient. Different patients can respond very differently to the same medication or treatment, and much of these differential treatment responses can be tied to the patient's genome.

For example, one patient might have a particular enzyme that rapidly degrades a specific drug, making that drug less effective. Another patient might have a version of the enzyme that works much slower, allowing the drug to remain in the patient's sysem for a longer time, making it more effective.

Predicting the type and dosage of medication most appropriate for a specific patient from their genome sequence is called "pharmacogenomics", and it is one important area in which phenotype prediction is being used to advance 'personalized medicine'.

In this case, the 'response variable' is tretment efficacy (how well a specific treatment protocol is expected to work for the patient), and the 'explanatory variables' are the patient's genome. Predicting the patient's treatment response from their genome allows doctors to tailor treatments for each specific patient, rather than relying on general guidelines that might not be relevant for every individual.

Agriculture

The most common use of phenotype prediction in the agricultural sciences is the selection of individuals for use in plant and animal breeding programs. Often called "genomic selection", the 'phenotypic trait' we would like to predict here is essentially 'utility in a particular breeding program'. Of course, this phenotype will vary greatly, depending on the particular goals of the breeding program! What we'd like to accomplish is, given a particular breeding goal and potential individuals to use in the breeding program, we'd like to select the 'best' individuals to accomplish the goals of the breeding program 'most efficiently'.

When the trait we'd like to select for in our breeding program has a very simple genetic basis (ie, it is controlled by a very small number of genes), we can easily use traditional breeding methods or transgenic approaches to 'transfer' the genes controlling the trait from one individual to another, thereby 'transferring' the phenotypic trait.

But for many complex phenotypic traits controlled by a very large number of genes, it may be difficult or impossible to even determine which particular genes impact the trait of interest, let alone effectively 'transfer' the trait from one individual to another.

By building a model that can predict an individual's phenotype from its genome, breeders are able to 'screen' individuals for traits of interest very rapidly using only their genome sequences. This approach can radically speed up plant and animal breeding programs, producing new lines with complex desirable traits without having to painstakingly investigate the specific genetic basis for the traits of interest.

Basic Biology

All life on earth descended from a single common ancestor that arose billions of years ago and diversified over time to produce the dizzying array of biodiversity that we see around us. Ultimately, then, all heritable biodiversity can be traced back to mutations in the genome sequence. Understanding how changes in the genome impact phenotypic diversity is therefore fundamental to understanding how life on earth works.

Predicting an organism's phenotype from its genome is an important tool for helping biologists untangle the complex relationship between the genome and the way an organism looks and acts.

For example, if a simple linear model is sufficient to accurately predict a phenotypic trait from the organism's genome, that says something about the genomic 'structure' of the trait - it says that the trait is 'additive' (each gene contributes 'a little bit' to the phenotypic trait, and those contributions are 'independent' of one another).

In contrast, if a simple linear model is insufficient to accurately predict an individual's phenotype from its genome, it tells us that the 'genetic basis' for the trait is more complex; it may include 'interactions' among multiple genes or be highly impacted by environmental factors.

By comparing different phenotype prediction models and investigating the best-fit values of model parameters, researchers can learn more about how the genome 'works' to produce a variety of phenotypes. This gives us important insights into how life on earth has been shaped by genome evolution.
