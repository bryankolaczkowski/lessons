PHENOTYPE PREDICTION

your genome impacts how you look and act... but how?

What is phenotype prediction?

Predicting how you look and act, based on your genome and environment.

We all look and act a little differently. We have different heights, hair and eye colors, blood types, etc. We also respond differently to our environment. We might require different amounts of sleep, have different natural energy levels, have allergies to different foods, respond differently to pharmaceuticals, etc.

Collectively, how an organism looks and acts can be referred to as the organism's "phenotype". The phenotype generally includes everything you can measure about an organism's morphology, physiology and behavior.

There are two major factors that impact an organism's phenotype:

  • its genome
  • its environment
An organism's genome is its DNA inherited from its parents and modified by mutations. Its environment is everything else.

Typically, we think of the genomic component as the heritable factors that impact the way an organism looks and acts. The genome is 'passed on' (with modification) from parent to offspring. If the genome strongly determines a particular aspect of the phenotype (called a "trait"), that trait is heritable; it can be 'inherited' from the organism's parents.

More 'quantitatively', we can talk about the "heritability" of a phenotypic trait - the extent to which the trait is determined by the organism's genome. Or, more broadly, the extent to which the child's phenotype is 'similar to' the phenotype of its parents (after controlling for any environmental determination).

Phenotype prediction is using information about an organism's genome and environment to predict a specific quantitative or qualitative aspect of its phenotype.

For example, let's say we'd like to predict a person's height. The response variable is "height", a 'quantitative trait' (ie, a number). The potential explanatory variables might be the person's genome and information about the person's current and past environments, such as the country the person grew up in, the person's income, information about their dietary and exercise habits, and any diseases they may have or may have had in the past.

We can then build a model relating the explanatory variables (genome, environment) to the response variable (height). The model is just a function: 

height = f(genome, environment)

Which can be a simple linear model or any arbitrary function that can be approximated by a neural network.

Given enough 'training examples' (people with known height, genome and enviornmental variables), we can 'learn' the 'trainable parameters' of the model.

Once trained, we can use the model to predict any individual's height from their genome and environment.

In this example, we used both the genome and the environment to predict an individual's phenotype. This would be a case of 'general' phenotype prediction.

In many cases, researchers are interested in predicting the phenotype using only the organism's genome; this is sometimes called "genomic prediction" (which is maybe weird, because we are not trying to predict the genome!). Phenotype prediction using only the genome is typically much more straight-forward than trying to deal with data about the individual's environment, which can be complex and difficult to measure and analyze effectively. Predicting an organism's phenotypic traits from its genome has many important applications throughout the agricultural and life sciences.

How is phenotype prediction used?

In medical research, agriculture and basic biology.

Medical Research

There are two primary applications of phenotype prediction in the biomedical sciences

  • disease risk prediction
  • personalized medicine

Disease risk prediction is the prediction of an individual's risk for developing a particular disease, based on the person's genome.

Personalized medicine is the tailoring of medical treatment to the individual patient.

Agriculture

The most common use of phenotype prediction in the agricultural sciences is the selection of individuals for use in plant and animal breeding programs. Often called "genomic selection", the 'phenotypic trait' we would like to predict here is essentially 'utility in a particular breeding program'. Of course, this phenotype will vary greatly, depending on the particular goals of the breeding program! What we'd like to accomplish is, given a particular breeding goal and potential individuals to use in the breeding program, we'd like to select the 'best' individuals to accomplish the goals of the breeding program 'most efficiently'.

When the trait we'd like to select for in our breeding program has a very simple genetic basis (ie, it is controlled by a very small number of genes), we can easily use traditional breeding methods or transgenic approaches to 'transfer' the genes controlling the trait from one individual to another, thereby 'transferring' the phenotypic trait.

But for many complex phenotypic traits controlled by a very large number of genes, it may be difficult or impossible to even determine which particular genes impact the trait of interest, let alone effectively 'transfer' the trait from one individual to another.

By building a model that can predict an individual's phenotype from its genome, breeders are able to 'screen' individuals for traits of interest very rapidly using only their genome sequences. This approach can radically speed up plant and animal breeding programs, producing new lines with complex desirable traits without having to painstakingly investigate the specific genetic basis for the traits of interest.
